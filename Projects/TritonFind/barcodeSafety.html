What does the barcode on the back of my UCSD ID even do?

First clarification, the barcode on the back of your ID does NOT correspond your PID.

Your PID starts with a letter (usually "A"), and grants you access to most your UCSD accounts and services.

The barcode on the back of your ID is all numbers (starts with "21822...") and helps you access some services from UCSD. It is completely unique to your current physical ID (if you lose your ID, your new ID would have a new barcode number).

Second, here is a list that TritonFind has created on all the possible uses for the barcode on the back of your ID:

  1. Register for Sun God ( archived link)
  2. Access your UCSD Library Account
  3. Reserve a group study room at the library
  4. Access other miscellaneous library services

Most of these services require you provide other forms of identification to utilize. For example, Sun God registration require PID and full name, Library Accounts require a PIN (if it is registered), and group study rooms require a valid @ucsd.edu email.

Some services, however, only require the barcode. Which is why TritonFind takes some extra steps to keep your code safe.

How does TritonFind keep my barcode safe?

TritonFind has to store barcode information in a database, in order to provide the unique services that it performs.

TritonFind "salts and hashes" barcode information. So, even if your barcode is "218230596789123", it may appear on TritonFind's database as:

"0e48dad2000ba9b8ca4483e9e7a28d36a88ae890712f2bd0c80d3bee89b47969"

This is done for two reasons:

  1. To prevent the TritonFind team from accidentally viewing users' barcode data.
  2. To impede hacker's attempts from harming our users.

This process is a hash and salt approach. It's similar to how many companies and businesses protect their users' passwords.

Is TritonFind 100% secure?

For TritonFind's purpose, it is secure enough.

This is what would have to go wrong in order for TritonFind's security to be compromised:

  1. An intruder gains access to the server that TritonFind is ran on.
  2. They then break into and steal information from TritonFind's database.
  3. Then, they spend a ton of resources into reversing the hashing/salting algorithms, or attempt a brute-force algorithm to decode the hash.
  4. Finally, they might have some form of usable data from only some users.

Then, what could a hacker use with this data?

Not much, really. Since they lack other information about you (full names, PID, passwords, etc), they can't access services that the barcode provides.
They could, however, gain unauthorized access some UCSD services. Go here to see how you can protect yourself in case of a breach such as this. Also, TritonFind will attempt to contact and give any form of aid to users if an event like that happens.


But what would be an easier option for a hacker who wants to cause harm?

Well, maybe they could simply brute force the UC Systemwide Library ILL service, since no PIN is required (even though other colleges, like UC Davis and UC Santa Barbara, require PIN's), a python script could easily break into and scrape student's personal information (like email address and home address). This would certainly be easier than breaking into TritonFind's system, would cause more damage, and quite less complex.

But make no doubt, even though there might be bigger and easier targets out than TritonFind, the TritonFind team is still very, very committed to keeping our service safe and secure. Our team strives to keep TritonFind's security measures one step ahead, and always keeps the best interests of our users.
